*Most contrastive losses are a cosine similarity plus a patch that stops it from collapsing. SimO₂ removes the patch by changing the similarity. This is the loss on its own terms, what it brings that the others don't, and, just as plainly, where the "so what" is weaker. Two figures run live in your browser.*

Line up the contrastive zoo and you notice every member is carrying a crutch.
InfoNCE and SimCLR keep the representation from collapsing to a point with
**sampled negatives**; MoCo swaps those for a **momentum queue**; BYOL and SimSiam
drop negatives but bolt on a **predictor head and a stop-gradient** to manufacture
asymmetry; VICReg adds a **variance hinge**; Barlow Twins a **cross-correlation
penalty**. Different machinery, same job: cosine similarity, left alone, has a
trivial minimiser, send every embedding to the same vector, and each method
adds a mechanism whose whole purpose is to forbid that.

What if the similarity simply didn't have the failure mode? Then you would not
need the patch. That is the entire idea behind **SimO₂**.

## Four routes around collapse

Why does every member of the zoo need a crutch in the first place? Because
**collapse** is not an edge case; it is the global optimum. Pull positives
together with nothing pushing back and the constant map, every input sent to one
vector, wins outright. Nor is uniformity a default you can lean on: even a
random, untrained deep network already bunches its normalized outputs into a
narrow angular cone, the cone effect
[the Welch-bound post](/blog/welch-bound-good-latent-space/) demonstrates live,
and depth only tightens it. So spread has to be manufactured, and two decades of
contrastive learning, from the margin losses through InfoNCE's softmax over
negatives to the predictor-and-stop-gradient and variance-penalty families, are
at heart four ways of manufacturing it:

| route | the mechanism it adds | examples |
|---|---|---|
| sampled negatives | push against other samples (+ temperature) | SimCLR, MoCo, InfoNCE |
| architectural asymmetry | predictor head + stop-gradient | BYOL, SimSiam |
| decorrelation / variance | penalise correlated features | Barlow Twins, VICReg |
| **repulsive similarity** | the kernel repels *by construction* | **SimO₂** (this post) |

The first three each add a hyperparameter and a moving part to compensate for
something cosine lacks. The fourth asks the different question: what if the
*similarity itself* couldn't collapse? Then there is nothing to patch, which is
where the ⵟ-kernel comes in.

## The ⵟ-kernel

Feel cosine's indifference from the inside first. Two embeddings that *should*
differ start drifting toward the same point, and cosine feels nothing: the score
just slides comfortably up toward 1, no alarm, no resistance, and total
co-location sits there as a perfectly acceptable minimum. Now ask what kind of
similarity would refuse. One with a $1/\text{distance}^2$ core, the same
inverse-square wells that let
[the editing posts](/blog/edit-a-network-by-hand/) treat a prototype as a mass
pulling on its neighborhood, changes the character of the landscape entirely:
as the distance between two points goes to zero the similarity diverges, so
co-locating two things that should differ costs infinitely much. Collapse stops
being a minimum and becomes a wall.

The biased **ⵟ-kernel** (from the
[ⵟ-kernel work](/blog/attention-is-a-kernel/) of Bouhsine, 2026) is that wall
written down:

$$
k_{b,\varepsilon}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) = \frac{(\langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle + b)^2}{\varepsilon + \lVert\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}\rVert^2}, \qquad b > 0,\ \varepsilon > 0,
$$

a polynomial **alignment** numerator over an inverse-multiquadric **distance**
denominator. On the unit sphere
$\lVert\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}\rVert^2 = 2 - 2S$ with $S = \langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle$, so it reduces to a
single function of the cosine,

$$
k^{\circ}_{b,\varepsilon}(S) = \frac{(S + b)^2}{\varepsilon + 2 - 2S},
$$

computed in one broadcast over the Gram matrix, at the cost of a cosine. For
$b > 0$ this kernel is Mercer, strictly positive-definite, characteristic and
universal, with a closed-form RKHS norm; and its softening $\varepsilon$ is no
temperature knob, it reshapes the RKHS the kernel induces, so turning it moves
the function class you learn in, not just an operating point. But the property
that matters here is visible just from the shape of the curve.

## The loss

How do you turn that curve into a loss? The kernel has already made the
decision: same-class pairs should score high (push $S \to 1$), different-class
pairs should score low (push $S$ toward the kernel's zero). SimO₂ just writes
that down, two per-pair terms gated by class masks,

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{same}}(i,j) = \frac{1}{1 + k_{ij}}, \qquad
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{diff}}(i,j) = 1 + k_{ij},
$$

averaged within and across classes into a single objective trained by the
**AFCL** algorithm (anchor-free contrastive learning):

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{AFCL}} = \lambda_+\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{intra}} + \lambda_-\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{inter}}.
$$

No temperature. No sampled negatives, no memory queue. No predictor, no
stop-gradient, no momentum encoder. No variance, covariance, or whitening
penalty. Every cross-class pair enters through a symmetric mask; the kernel does
the work the patches used to do. Here is what that buys, in order of how
defensible the claim is.

## What happens when two embeddings try to collapse?

The wall is now a loss term, so run the failure the whole zoo was built to
prevent straight into it. Let two different-class embeddings drift toward the
same point: the $\lVert\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}\rVert^2$ in the denominator shrinks
toward zero, $k$ blows up, and the inter-class term $1 + k$ punishes exactly
that. The closer two embeddings that should differ get, the harder the loss
shoves them apart.
Repulsion is not added; it is the kernel. No negatives to mine, no asymmetry to
engineer, no auxiliary loss to weight.

Watch it happen, self-supervised, on a shared encoder. Left: alignment only,
pull each sample's two augmented views together, with nothing pushing back. The
encoder takes the shortcut every alignment-only objective takes and **collapses**,
mapping every sample onto one direction. Right: the same encoder, same data, but
the ⵟ-kernel's inter term supplies repulsion, **no sampled negatives, no
predictor, no variance penalty**, and the embeddings spread across the circle.

## If repulsion has a target, who chose it?

Repulsion alone only says "not here." The surprising part of the ⵟ-kernel's
version is that it also says *where to go instead*: the curve's zero sits at
$S = -b$, so pushing $k$ down does not fling different-class pairs toward the
degenerate antipode, the "opposite is different" failure
[cosine losses fall into](/blog/opposite-is-not-different/), it parks them at a
cosine *you picked*. The math underneath: the inter-class loss is, to leading
order, the **biased frame potential**
$\sum_{i\neq j}(\langle\mathbf{e}_i,\mathbf{e}_j\rangle + b)^2$, minimised not at the rank-collapsing
antipode but at $\langle\mathbf{e}_i,\mathbf{e}_j\rangle = -b$. So the equilibrium it settles into
is not an emergent accident; it is closed-form and **steerable by the single
scalar $b$.**

If that is really true, it makes a prediction you can watch, and it is not a
gentle one: somewhere as you raise $b$, the geometry should not slide, it should
*jump*, snapping between qualitatively different arrangements at a sharp
threshold. Drag $b$ below and watch for the snap:

<SimoEquilibrium caption="C class centroids settling under the biased frame potential, live. The single scalar b steers the equilibrium: below b_c = 1/[2(C−1)] the vectors fan into an orthogonal frame; above 1/(C−1) they snap to the regular simplex (the max-margin, >90° configuration). Watch the regime label and the bifurcation flip as you cross b_c. This designability, geometry by a scalar, is what no sampled-negative or variance-penalty loss can expose." />

What you just watched has a closed form. For $C$ classes in $d$ dimensions
($C \le d+1$):

- below the bifurcation $b_c = \dfrac{1}{2(C-1)}$ → an **orthogonal frame**;
- above the simplex threshold $\dfrac{1}{C-1}$ → the **regular simplex**, the
  max-margin configuration with angle $\arccos\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{C-1}\right) > 90°$,
  the same simplex ETF that
  [neural collapse](/blog/three-states-of-information/) arrives at from the
  supervised side;
- in between → an interior equiangular frame at $-b$.

(For $C > d$ the analogous minimiser is a unit-norm tight frame at the
[Welch bound](/blog/welch-bound-good-latent-space/).) In the analysis so far
that yields a parameter-free margin radius $r = \sqrt{C/(2(C-1))}$ for
nearest-centroid classification at the simplex, what the accompanying paper
develops as a certified radius, strictly larger than the $\sqrt{2}/2$ an
orthogonal-margin loss could reach.

The point is not the specific numbers; it is that **embedding geometry becomes a
control problem.** No standard contrastive loss hands you an equilibrium you can
dial.

## Can gradient descent be trusted to find it?

A closed-form optimum is worth little if training gets stuck somewhere else, and
there is a potential methodological first hiding here. Every prior proof that a
contrastive or neural-collapse objective has no bad local minima relaxes the
problem to a **convex program in the Gram matrix**. The ⵟ-kernel's rational
denominator makes that relaxation structurally unavailable, and the accompanying
paper argues the landscape is benign anyway, directly on the product of spheres:
in the analysis so far, every non-simplex critical orbit is a strict saddle, so
gradient descent from almost any initialisation should reach the simplex. If
that analysis holds up, a non-convex-Gram loss with a global-convergence story
is not something the cosine families offer.

## The experiment

Theory aside, here is a small lab: a shared encoder, four contrastive losses
trained side by side on the same dataset and initialisation, **live in your
browser** (the gradients are checked against finite differences).
[Untangling the Moons](/blog/untangling-the-moons/) raced the whole contrastive
lineage this way; this race is narrower and asks one question, whether the
kernel alone can match the patched losses, with SimO₂ in the fourth lane.

Watch the geometry diverge. Alignment-only pulls each class tight but, with
nothing pushing back, leaves the classes **crowded**, no margin. SupCon and
SigLIP separate them, paying for it with a temperature and in-batch negatives.
SimO₂/AFCL reaches the **same max-margin simplex** with **neither**, the
repulsion is the kernel. Switch the dataset (two-moons, rings, spirals, four
classes), change `n` or the seed, **Step** through it, or just let it race.

## The honest "so what"

None of the above says **SimO₂ beats InfoNCE, VICReg, or BYOL on accuracy.** It
doesn't, not because it loses, but because that is a different claim with a
different burden of proof, and the downstream-win experiments are not yet done.
If a reviewer asks "why should I switch losses," the truthful answer is
**controllability and the analysis**, repulsion by construction and a designable
equilibrium the accompanying paper works to certify, not a leaderboard. Pitch it
as a benchmark result and it is unfinished; pitch it as mechanism and designable
geometry and it is a genuinely distinct fourth route. I'd rather state that plainly than oversell it.

The one-line version: **SimO₂ gives you collapse prevention as an intrinsic,
kernel-geometric property, with a closed-form equilibrium you steer by a scalar
bias, repulsion and geometry by construction, not by sampling, regularisation, or
asymmetry.** Pick the bias, get the frame; no patch required.
