*Most contrastive losses are a cosine similarity plus a patch that stops it from collapsing. SimO₂ removes the patch by changing the similarity. This is the loss on its own terms — what it brings that the others don't, and, just as plainly, where the "so what" is weaker. Two figures run live in your browser.*

Line up the contrastive zoo and you notice every member is carrying a crutch.
InfoNCE and SimCLR keep the representation from collapsing to a point with
**sampled negatives**; MoCo swaps those for a **momentum queue**; BYOL and SimSiam
drop negatives but bolt on a **predictor head and a stop-gradient** to manufacture
asymmetry; VICReg adds a **variance hinge**; Barlow Twins a **cross-correlation
penalty**. Different machinery, same job: cosine similarity, left alone, has a
trivial minimiser — send every embedding to the same vector — and each method
adds a mechanism whose whole purpose is to forbid that.

What if the similarity simply didn't have the failure mode? Then you would not
need the patch. That is the entire idea behind **SimO₂**.

## A short history of not collapsing

Contrastive learning has one recurring enemy, and most of its history is the
story of beating it: **collapse**, the trivial solution where the encoder sends
everything to one vector. Pull positives together with nothing pushing back and
that constant map *is* the global optimum. And uniformity is not a default you can
lean on — push Gaussian inputs through a *random, untrained* deep network and the
outputs already bunch into a narrow cone instead of spreading over the sphere.
Add layers and the cone tightens:

<ConeEffect caption="Outputs of a random, untrained ReLU network, L2-normalized to the circle. Even with no training, depth squeezes the outputs into a narrow angular cone (the 'cone effect') — drag the depth slider and watch the half-width shrink. 'Uniform on the sphere' is something a contrastive objective has to manufacture, not a starting point. Re-roll for a fresh random network." />

So every era of contrastive learning is, at heart, a different way to manufacture
that spread. The genealogy:

- **Metric learning (2006–2015).** The contrastive loss of Hadsell, Chopra &
  LeCun and FaceNet's triplet loss pull matched pairs together and push mismatched
  pairs apart *past a margin* — explicit negatives, explicit margin.
- **InfoNCE (2018).** Instance-discrimination and CPC make every sample its own
  class and turn the push-apart into a softmax over negatives, scaled by a
  **temperature**.
- **The 2020 scale-up.** SimCLR makes it work with strong augmentations and *large
  batches of in-batch negatives*; MoCo replaces the batch with a *momentum queue*.
- **Negative-free (2020–2021).** BYOL drops negatives entirely and blocks collapse
  with an *asymmetric predictor + EMA target*; SimSiam shows the *stop-gradient* is
  the load-bearing part.
- **Redundancy reduction (2021–2022).** Barlow Twins and VICReg avoid both
  negatives and asymmetry by penalising *correlation between feature dimensions* —
  a variance/decorrelation term.
- **Supervised & multimodal.** SupCon uses all same-class positives; CLIP and
  SigLIP align two modalities with the same machinery.

Squint and the pattern is unmistakable. The underlying similarity is almost
always **cosine**, cosine alone collapses, so each family bolts on a different
mechanism to forbid it. There are essentially **four routes**:

| route | the mechanism it adds | examples |
|---|---|---|
| sampled negatives | push against other samples (+ temperature) | SimCLR, MoCo, InfoNCE |
| architectural asymmetry | predictor head + stop-gradient | BYOL, SimSiam |
| decorrelation / variance | penalise correlated features | Barlow Twins, VICReg |
| **repulsive similarity** | the kernel repels *by construction* | **SimO₂** (this post) |

The first three each add a hyperparameter and a moving part to compensate for
something cosine lacks. The fourth asks the different question: what if the
*similarity itself* couldn't collapse? Then there is nothing to patch — which is
where the ⵟ-kernel comes in.

## The ⵟ-kernel

SimO₂ is built on the biased **ⵟ-kernel** (from the
[ⵟ-kernel work](/blog/attention-is-a-kernel/) of Bouhsine, 2026):

$$
k_{b,\varepsilon}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) = \frac{(\langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle + b)^2}{\varepsilon + \lVert\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}\rVert^2}, \qquad b > 0,\ \varepsilon > 0,
$$

a rational similarity: a polynomial **alignment** numerator over an
inverse-multiquadric **distance** denominator. On the unit sphere
$\lVert\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}\rVert^2 = 2 - 2S$ with $S = \langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle$, so it reduces to a
single function of the cosine,

$$
k^{\circ}_{b,\varepsilon}(S) = \frac{(S + b)^2}{\varepsilon + 2 - 2S},
$$

computed in one broadcast over the Gram matrix, at the cost of a cosine. For
$b > 0$ this kernel is Mercer, strictly positive-definite, characteristic and
universal, with a closed-form RKHS norm — but the property that matters here is
visible just from the shape of the curve.

## The loss

Proposition: same-class pairs want $k$ large (push $S \to 1$); different-class
pairs want $k$ small (push $S$ to the kernel's minimum). SimO₂ inverts that into
two per-pair terms, gated by class masks,

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{same}}(i,j) = \frac{1}{1 + k_{ij}}, \qquad
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{diff}}(i,j) = 1 + k_{ij},
$$

averaged within and across classes into a single objective trained by the
**AFCL** algorithm (anchor-free contrastive learning):

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{AFCL}} = \lambda_+\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{intra}} + \lambda_-\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{inter}}.
$$

No temperature. No sampled negatives, no memory queue. No predictor, no
stop-gradient, no momentum encoder. No variance, covariance, or whitening
penalty. Every cross-class pair enters through a symmetric mask; the kernel does
the work the patches used to do. Here is what that buys, in order of how
defensible the claim is.

## 1. Collapse prevention is intrinsic to the similarity

This is the real differentiator. There are, roughly, four ways to stop a
representation from collapsing: sampled negatives (SimCLR), a variance/decorrelation
penalty (VICReg, Barlow Twins), an architectural asymmetry (BYOL's
predictor + EMA), and — the fourth route — **a similarity that is repulsive by
construction.** SimO₂ is that fourth route. The $\lVert\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}\rVert^2$ in
the denominator means two embeddings that march toward the same point drive
$k \to$ large, and the inter-class term $1 + k$ punishes exactly that. Repulsion
is not added; it is the kernel. No negatives to mine, no asymmetry to engineer,
no auxiliary loss to weight.

Watch it happen, self-supervised, on a shared encoder. Left: alignment only —
pull each sample's two augmented views together, with nothing pushing back. The
encoder takes the shortcut every alignment-only objective takes and **collapses**,
mapping every sample onto one direction. Right: the same encoder, same data, but
the ⵟ-kernel's inter term supplies repulsion — **no sampled negatives, no
predictor, no variance penalty** — and the embeddings spread across the circle.

## 2. A closed-form equilibrium you can steer with one scalar

The inter-class loss is, to leading order, the **biased frame potential**
$\sum_{i\neq j}(\langle\mathbf{e}_i,\mathbf{e}_j\rangle + b)^2$ — minimised not at the rank-collapsing
antipode but at $\langle\mathbf{e}_i,\mathbf{e}_j\rangle = -b$. And the equilibrium it settles into
is not an emergent accident; it is closed-form and **steerable by the single
scalar $b$.** For $C$ classes in $d$ dimensions ($C \le d+1$):

- below the bifurcation $b_c = \dfrac{1}{2(C-1)}$ → an **orthogonal frame**;
- above the simplex threshold $\dfrac{1}{C-1}$ → the **regular simplex**, the
  max-margin configuration with angle $\arccos\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{C-1}\right) > 90°$;
- in between → an interior equiangular frame at $-b$.

(For $C > d$ the analogous minimiser is a unit-norm tight frame at the Welch
bound.) That gives a parameter-free certified radius
$r = \sqrt{C/(2(C-1))}$ for nearest-centroid classification at the simplex —
strictly larger than the $\sqrt{2}/2$ an orthogonal-margin loss could reach.

The point is not the specific numbers; it is that **embedding geometry becomes a
control problem.** No standard contrastive loss hands you an equilibrium you can
dial. Drag $b$ below:

## 3. A benign landscape *outside* the convex-Gram regime

There is a methodological first hiding here. Every prior proof that a contrastive
or neural-collapse objective has no bad local minima relaxes the problem to a
**convex program in the Gram matrix**. The ⵟ-kernel's rational denominator makes
that relaxation structurally unavailable — and SimO₂'s landscape is proven benign
anyway, directly on the product of spheres: every non-simplex critical orbit is a
strict saddle, so gradient descent from almost any initialisation reaches the
simplex. A non-convex-Gram loss with a clean global-convergence guarantee is not
something the cosine families offer.

## 4. ε moves the function class, not just the scale

A softmax temperature rescales logits — it sharpens, nothing more. The kernel
regulariser $\varepsilon$ is different in kind: it changes the **RKHS** the kernel
induces, so turning it moves the *function class* you are learning in, not just
the operating point. It is a principled kernel parameter with Mercer meaning, not
a free knob.

## The experiment

Theory aside, here is a small lab: a shared encoder, four contrastive losses
trained side by side on the same dataset and initialisation, **all in your
browser**. It runs on a tiny reverse-mode autodiff engine
([`nanograd`](https://github.com/mlnomadpy/blog/tree/master/src/components/viz/engine))
written for this blog — which matters, because it means each loss is just its
*forward* formula and the gradients come for free. That is how **SupCon's softmax
contrastive loss** is in the race at all: no hand-derived gradient, just autodiff
(checked against finite differences before it shipped).

Watch the geometry diverge. Alignment-only pulls each class tight but, with
nothing pushing back, leaves the classes **crowded** — no margin. SupCon and
SigLIP separate them, paying for it with a temperature and in-batch negatives.
SimO₂/AFCL reaches the **same max-margin simplex** with **neither** — the
repulsion is the kernel. Switch the dataset (two-moons, rings, spirals, four
classes), change `n` or the seed, **Step** through it, or just let it race.

## The honest "so what"

None of the above says **SimO₂ beats InfoNCE, VICReg, or BYOL on accuracy.** It
doesn't — not because it loses, but because that is a different claim with a
different burden of proof, and the downstream-win experiments are not yet done.
If a reviewer asks "why should I switch losses," the truthful answer is
**controllability and the proof** — repulsion and a designable, certified
equilibrium *by construction* — not a leaderboard. Pitch it as a benchmark result
and it is unfinished; pitch it as mechanism and provable geometry and it is a
genuinely distinct fourth route. I'd rather state that plainly than oversell it.

The one-line version: **SimO₂ gives you collapse prevention as an intrinsic,
kernel-geometric property, with a closed-form equilibrium you steer by a scalar
bias — repulsion and geometry by construction, not by sampling, regularisation, or
asymmetry.** Pick the bias, get the frame; no patch required.
