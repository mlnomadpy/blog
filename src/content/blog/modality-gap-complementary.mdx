You have two modalities of the same thing, a waveform and its spectrogram, an
image and its caption, a scan and its report, and you want one space that holds
both. In 2024 the reflex was automatic: reach for contrastive learning. Encode
each modality, pull matched pairs together, push mismatched pairs apart, and you
get a shared multimodal space for free.

Then you notice the embeddings won't come together, settling instead into two
separated cones, the **modality gap**, the thing you just watched open, and you
start hunting for the trick that closes it. This post is about why that hunt is
usually the wrong instinct. The question to settle *before* you pick any objective
is not "how do I close the gap." It is: **what is this space for?** Is its job to
*separate* concepts or to *represent* them? Those are different jobs. They pull the
geometry in opposite directions, and the gap is just a readout of which one you
chose.

## Two jobs for a latent space

Every latent space is asked to do one of two things, and the two fight.

**Separate.** This is the classifier's job: discrimination. To put a clean
boundary between cat and dog, the space has to collapse every cat onto roughly
one place and every dog onto another, and *throw away* everything that does not
help tell them apart, the pose, the lighting, the breed, the particular cat. A
classifier is rewarded for forgetting. Anything that creates ambiguity near the
boundary is noise to be discarded. Done well, the classes end up as tight
clusters with empty space between them, and that empty space is the point.

**Represent.** This is the reconstructor's job: denoising, generation, retrieval,
faithful recall. To rebuild or retrieve the input you have to *keep* the input,
all of it, including the parts that create ambiguity, because you have to put them
back or tell two near-identical things apart. A good representer is rewarded for
remembering. It cannot collapse within-class variation, because that variation
*is* the thing it has to reproduce.

These are not two ends of a dial you can set halfway. The information you discard
to separate cleanly is exactly the information you need to represent faithfully.
A space optimized to forget cannot also remember. You pick one.

## Classify or retrieve

Here is the same axis in the words of someone deciding what to *do* with a vector
database. You will use the space for one of two things.

**Classification** is separation. You want the category, and you want it robust to
everything that isn't the category. The latent space earns its keep by discarding
exactly the variation that would make the boundary fuzzy. Forgetting is the
feature.

**Retrieval** is representation. You want to find *this* item, the one record
that differs from its neighbours in the way that matters. That means keeping the
fine, within-category detail that classification is paid to destroy. Two
documents in the same class, two faces of the same person, two X-rays of the same
condition: a classifier wants them identical; retrieval needs them distinct.

The trap is that one word, *retrieval*, hides both jobs. CLIP-style cross-modal
**matching**, "find the caption that goes with this image", is not really
retrieval in the representing sense. It is classification wearing a retrieval
costume: you only ever wanted the shared concept, so discarding everything else is
fine, and contrastive is exactly right. But the moment retrieval means
*discriminate within a concept*, rank the near-duplicates, find the one that's
subtly different, you are representing, and a separating space has already thrown
away the only information that could rank them. Same nominal task, opposite sides
of the axis.

So the deciding question is never the modality, and never even the task name. It
is the job: separate or represent.

## The smallest experiment

Talk is cheap. Here is the whole argument in the smallest setup I could build,
no radio, no transformers, just **four distributions** and one linear projector
per modality. Everything from here runs live in your browser.

There are **two classes**, $c \in \{0, 1\}$. There is a **within-class factor**
$t$, a position along each cluster, drawn *independently* for each modality. So
a matched pair shares its class and *nothing else*: $t$ is modality-unique. Each
modality is the same 2-D structure pushed into 16 dimensions by a different random
map. Throughout, **shape is the class** (○ vs ✕) and **colour is the modality**
(A vs B): a point's shape tells you *what it is*, its colour *which view it came
from*.

<ModalityData caption="The four distributions: two modalities (colour) × two classes (shape ○ / ✕), each shown via its top-2 principal components. The class is shared across the modalities; the within-class position is drawn independently for each, so beyond the class label there is nothing the two views agree on." />

Now give every model the *same* linear projector down to a 2-D bottleneck, so
capacity is held fixed and only the **objective** can differ, and train two ways.
One **represents**: reconstruct each modality's own input (a linear autoencoder,
whose optimum is just PCA). One **separates**: align the two modalities with the
SigLIP pairwise-sigmoid loss. Then ask each space to do both jobs: recover the
class (the separable slice) and recover $t$ (the representable whole).

The widget below *is* the experiment. It generates the four distributions once,
computes the representing space in closed form, and trains the separating space by
**real gradient descent, Adam on a SigLIP loss, the gradients hand-derived and
checked against finite differences, live, no server**. Hit train and watch the
separating space tighten each class to a point (recover-t R² → 0) while the
representing space keeps its spread (R² ≈ 0.97). Both still **classify** at 100%,
that is the one job a separating space is built for. Flip the colour control to
"within-class $t$" to see directly what each space did with it.

Why does SigLIP discard $t$? Because $t$ carries no cross-modal agreement.
Modality A's within-class factor and modality B's are independent draws, so if the
projector kept $t$, a matched pair would land at *different* places and the
alignment loss would punish it. To maximize agreement the model must send every
same-class point to one spot, regardless of $t$, which is to say, it must throw
$t$ away. Separation is not a side effect of the loss. It *is* the loss. (It shows
up in retrieval, too: nearest-neighbour search *within* a class returns
$t$-similar items from the representing space and random ones from the separating
space.)

But notice what the separating space did *not* do here, it collapsed the
within-class factor, yet with a single linear head the two modalities still ended
up mostly aligned by class, only mildly apart. The famous *gap*, two modalities
flung into separate cones, is a second thing the same objective can do, and to
see it open in full we have to change the regime.

## Contrastive is separation in multimodal clothing

This generalizes past the toy. **Contrastive learning is a classifier over pairs.**
Pull matched together, push mismatched apart: formally it maximizes a bound on the
mutual information between the modalities, $I(X_1; X_2)$, so it keeps only what the
two views *agree* on and treats everything else as ambiguity to discard. It builds
a space that separates. **Joint reconstruction**, a masked or denoising
autoencoder over both modalities, has to model the joint $p(x, y)$: what the
views share, what each carries alone, and the structure that only appears when you
line them up. It cannot drop the ambiguous parts, because it has to put them back.
It builds a space that represents.

The clean way to name the pieces is Partial Information Decomposition
([Liang et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.12247)). The information two
modalities carry splits into three parts, not one: **redundancy** (what either
view alone tells you, the part both agree on), **uniqueness** (what only one
tells you), and **synergy** (what neither tells you alone, only the two together).
A separating objective keeps redundancy and is blind to uniqueness and synergy,
because those two are exactly the information that creates ambiguity at the
agreement boundary. In the toy, the class is redundancy and $t$ is uniqueness,
and you watched the separating space keep the first and destroy the second.

## The gap is the signature of a separating space

Opening the gap in full takes two ingredients the linear toy lacked. Swap the
single linear projector for **two small ReLU towers** (a couple of layers each,
depth is what gives the "cone effect" room to appear), and use a **sharper
temperature**. Keep everything else: the same two-class, two-modality signal,
SigLIP, and, the key move, **both towers start from the same initialization**,
so at step 0 the two clouds sit on top of each other. There is no gap to close,
because there is none.

That is precisely the **simulation that opens this post**. Scroll back up and hit
**restart** to run it again from a fresh init, and watch the sparkline: the gap
climbs from ~0.2 to well over 1 in the first hundred steps, while matched-pair
cosine *falls*, through zero and negative. And drag the **sharpness (1/τ)**
slider: at a soft temperature (≈1) the two modalities simply align and the gap
barely opens; the dramatic cones only appear once you sharpen it. The gap is a
choice of regime, not an inevitability, which is the honest caveat to keep in
mind before declaring any loss the winner. (The in-browser version backprops
through the two towers with the same hand-written autodiff and Adam;
[`gap.py`](https://github.com/mlnomadpy/blog/tree/master/scripts/jax-modality-gap)
is the JAX version.)

This reframes the gap completely. The classic finding is that CLIP settles image
and text into two separated cones
([Liang et al., 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.02053)), and the usual reading is
that something went wrong and the cones are a defect. Watch the dynamics and the
defect dissolves. Two encoders meet only through an agreement gradient. An
agreement gradient cares about one thing: that a matched pair scores higher than a
mismatched one. It does **not** care where on the sphere the two modalities sit, or
whether they sit together at all. So the objective is free to satisfy "matched
beats random" while sliding the two clouds into opposite cones, and with a sharp
temperature, that is exactly what it does. Matched-pair cosine ends up *negative*:
the pairs are pushed apart in absolute terms, still ranked above random, the gap
wide open.

"Two cones" is worth making literal, because that geometry is the whole point.
Embeddings are L2-normalized, so every point is a **unit vector, a direction**
from the origin. Each modality's directions don't scatter over the whole sphere;
they bundle into a narrow angular **wedge, a cone**. The modality gap is simply
the **angle between the two cones' axes**. The view below is the same deep-tower
run drawn this way: rays from the origin, a shaded wedge per modality, and the gap
marked as the angle where the axes diverge. Train it and watch the two cones swing
apart from a single shared bundle to opposite sides.

So the gap is not the disease. It is a symptom, and an honest one. In the linear
toy the separating objective showed its hand as collapse (the within-class factor
thrown away); with depth and temperature it shows up as geometry (two cones). Both
are the space telling you it was asked to separate. You can close the geometric gap
with tricks, and it still will not build a representer, because closing the gap
does not turn a separating space into a representing one. Joint reconstruction
never opens a gap in the first place, not because it closes gaps better, but
because it was never asked to separate. One shared decoder, no agreement pressure
manufacturing two cones. It does not bridge the modalities. It simply represents
them.

## So when does the gap hurt?

It hurts when your task is a representation task wearing other clothes.

- **Denoising, reconstruction, generation**, where the target is the whole signal,
  including the within-class detail. The maximal-hurt case.
- **Fine-grained retrieval, captioning, visual question answering, multimodal
  medical fusion**, where the answer turns on what only one modality shows, or on
  what two modalities say jointly. The discriminating detail is exactly the
  ambiguity a separating space throws away.

It is close to harmless when your task is genuinely a separation task.

- **Zero-shot classification and coarse cross-modal matching**, where you only ever
  wanted the agreed-upon concept. Here the geometric gap is cosmetic, and the
  contrastive recipe is not just fine but ideal. Discarding uniqueness becomes a
  feature, a form of relevance filtering, and the independent encoders give you
  modular embeddings that scale to web data.

That last point is the honest counterweight. A separating space's blindness to
uniqueness is the same property that lets you embed an image without its caption,
train on billions of loosely-paired examples, and do open-vocabulary matching. A
representing space buys synergy and pays in modularity: it wants all modalities
present, and it spends capacity modeling detail a matcher would gladly discard.
Neither is better in the abstract. One forgets on purpose; the other remembers on
purpose.

## The rule

Here is the rule, the one I wish I had before spending the compute to learn it.

> A latent space cannot both separate and represent. Classification asks it to
> forget what creates ambiguity; reconstruction and fine retrieval ask it to keep
> exactly that. Contrastive learning builds a space that separates; joint
> reconstruction builds a space that represents. The modality gap is not a bug,
> it is what a separating space looks like, and if your task needs the signal back,
> the gap is a sign you asked for separation when you needed representation.

The deciding variable is the job.

| your space's job is to… | so it keeps… | right objective | example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **separate** / classify (forget the ambiguous) | the agreed-upon concept, the intersection | contrastive (CLIP, SigLIP) | zero-shot, coarse matching |
| **represent** / retrieve (keep the ambiguous) | the whole signal, the union and the synergy | reconstruction (MAE, autoencoder) | denoising, fine retrieval, fusion |

We hit the wall of this rule on a real system, trying to denoise radio signals
with three complementary views, where contrastive burned compute confirming the
embeddings would not come together and a multimodal autoencoder quietly won with
less data. But that war story deserves its own post; the simulations here are the
principle underneath it, stripped to the smallest shape that still shows the
result, and small enough to run live, right here, while you read.
