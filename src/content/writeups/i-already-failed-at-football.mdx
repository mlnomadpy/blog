Some news arrived recently that should have been a trophy. The kind of result I
had worked toward for years, the kind that people around me treated as a big
deal, the kind that, if you had described it to me in advance, I would have
sworn would feel like winning. It arrived, and I read it twice, and I felt
roughly what you feel when a package you ordered shows up on time. Noted.
Logged. Next. Then I spent more time being confused about the silence than I
had spent on the news itself, because the machinery that is supposed to fire
did not fire, and I have felt it fire before, so I know I own one.

I have come to believe the machinery works perfectly. It just does not answer
to me. It answers to a contract that was signed on my behalf, decades ago, by
a place, before I could read.

## The brain does not meet the world, it meets a room

A brain is not one thing having one experience. It runs in states, and the
states decide what everything means. The same sentence lands as a joke, an
insult, or background noise depending on which brain shows up to hear it, and
which brain shows up is not something you vote on in the moment. It is set by
sleep and fear and company and by longer weather than that: by the room you
have been standing in for years. Emotion is not decoration on top of thought.
It is the operating state the thought runs in, and it decides which signals
get through at full volume and which arrive muted.

And here is the part that took me longest to accept: the room calibrates the
volume knobs, and it does it early. Two children can grow up on the same
planet and receive two different worlds, because perception is not a camera,
it is a filter bank tuned by whatever the surrounding people pointed at,
cheered for, and mourned. What your environment glorifies, your brain learns
to amplify. What your environment never mentions, your brain learns to pass
over as noise. Nobody asks you. The wiring is installed while you are busy
being a child, and by the time you can inspect it, it has been load-bearing
for years and calls itself "just how things are."

Happiness is wired the same way, and this is the piece I actually want to
write about. You do not get to choose what happiness attaches to. The room
chooses. It attaches the feeling of winning to whatever it worships, and it
does this before you have any say, and from then on your celebration
circuitry checks every win against that original list, and pays out only on
matches.

## The room I grew up in worshipped football

I was born in Morocco, which means the list had one entry, and the entry was
football. Not written anywhere, not enforced by anyone in particular, just
present the way weather is present. The men who were treated like meaning
itself were footballers. The dream every boy was issued at birth, the way you
are issued a name, was that one. The whole apparatus of glory, the shouting,
the flags, the grown men crying with joy in cafes, all of it pointed at one
kind of win, and my brain, being a child's brain in that room, did the only
thing brains do: it attached the feeling of triumph to the thing the room
said triumph was.

And I failed at it. Let me be precise: I was never close. The thing I failed
at, the one loss my nervous system registered as the real loss, the
foundational one, was being a football player. I find this genuinely funny
now, and I am telling it with a laugh, but I want you to see what it means
mechanically. The one contest my reward wiring recognized as the contest was
over before I was old enough to enter anything else. Everything I have done
since, all of it, has been played in tournaments my brain does not list.

So when the achievements came later, and some real ones did come, from a
field the boy in that room could not have named, they arrived at a window
that was never told to pay them. The win checks itself against the list. The
list says football. The win is not football. The window closes. People
congratulate you and you perform the smile honestly enough, because you
understand intellectually that this is a win, the way you understand
intellectually that a word in a foreign language is a compliment. But the
feeling pays out in a currency your childhood never issued, and the exchange
office in your head does not carry it. You cannot fully celebrate a win in a
game your room never told you was a game.

## The strange gift inside the defect

This sounds like a sad story, and part of it is, but I would be lying if I
stopped there, because the same wiring fault is also the closest thing I have
to a superpower, and I did not understand this for years.

Fear of failure needs something to threaten. It draws all its force from one
source: the feeling that this contest is the contest, that losing here is
losing, full stop. But my wiring never registered any of my actual work as
the contest. Deep in the accounting, the real loss already happened, decades
ago, on a football pitch I never reached. Everything since is played with
house money. So when I take on something that should be terrifying, a field I
have no credentials in, a problem above my weight, a bet that reasonable
people decline, the fear mostly does not come, and it is not because I am
brave. Bravery is feeling the stake and acting anyway. I simply do not feel
the stake, because the part of me that assigns stakes filed its one great
loss long ago and considers the matter closed. You cannot threaten a man with
losing a game he was never emotionally enrolled in.

That detachment reads, from the outside, like calm, like boldness, like
risk-tolerance. People have complimented me on nerves that are actually an
absence. And I have learned to use it honestly: I can start over, look
foolish, publish the strange idea, walk into the room I am unqualified for,
at a discount most people never get, because the pressure that stops them was
spent, in my case, on a dream I never chose. The room that broke my
celebration also broke my fear, with the same hammer, and only one of those
breaks turned out to be a wound.

## Owning the wiring

I want to be careful not to end this as a man explaining why his numbness is
secretly wisdom, because the honest ledger has both columns. The freedom is
real, and so is the cost. A life where wins do not pay is quietly expensive:
you skip the celebrations, and the people who love you notice before you do,
and motivation has to come from curiosity and stubbornness alone, because the
usual fuel, the anticipated joy of winning, is spoken for. I have written
about [what raised me instead](/blog/writeups/generation-zero/), and it is no
coincidence that none of it was reward.

And the wiring is mine to work on, not just to describe. Slowly, I am
learning to celebrate on purpose, the way you learn a language as an adult,
clumsily and by rule instead of by ear: stop, name the win out loud, let it
be one, borrow the joy on the faces of people who love me until my own
arrives, and it sometimes does arrive, late, small, but real, like the first
word you manage to think in the new language instead of translating. The
machinery can be retuned. It is just slow, because you are arguing with your
own foundations, and the foundations were poured by a whole country and left
to set for a childhood.

Here is the strangest part. When Morocco plays, I still feel it. The full
payout, the shouting, the joy with no translation step, the thing my own
milestones cannot buy. The machinery was never broken. It works exactly as
installed, and it still delivers, faithfully, to my first address. Until I
finish amending it, I will keep mailing my trophies to a house I no longer
live in.

Which leaves a question I cannot close this essay without noticing, because
the whole essay has quietly been sharpening it. If the wins do not pay, and
the fear does not push, then the two engines that drive most people are both
unplugged in me, and yet I have worked for years, past every stop sign, at
full throttle, on things nobody around me asked for. Something is running.
Something got a boy through school no one believed in, across an ocean, into
fields that did not exist in the room that raised him, and it was not the
hope of a trophy and it was not the whip of a deadline. So what keeps me
motivated? I have an answer, or at least I have caught the thing moving and
taken notes. That is the next essay.
