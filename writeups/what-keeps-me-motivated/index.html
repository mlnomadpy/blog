What Keeps Me Motivated?

July 8, 2026 · 11 min read

#notes#brain#motivation#emotions#environment#tamazgha#north-africa#personal

I ended the last writeup on a question I promised to answer, and I have been circling it since, because the answer is not one thing and some of it is not flattering. If the wins do not pay out and the fear does not push, what has been running the engine all these years? I sat down and did the inventory the way I would audit anything else: not what should fuel me, not what I would like to say fuels me at a dinner, but what is actually in the tank when I check. Here is what I found, noble parts and spare parts together, because an engine does not care where its fuel comes from, and neither, it turns out, do I.

The wiring I stopped fighting#

Start with the thing the last writeup left broken, because the first fuel is that same break, used on purpose. Being born in Tamazgha, the North Africa that is older than any of the borders drawn across it, installed a wiring I never chose: a reward system pointed at a game I lost before I could enter it, and underneath that, something older and more useful, a whole reflex built around the underdog, the doubted, the one nobody counted. I say Tamazgha and not just Morocco on purpose, because this is not one country’s temperament; walk the whole strip from the Atlantic to the desert and you meet the same mentality, the same stubborn traditions, the same instinct of a people used to being underestimated and quietly certain anyway.

And that certainty has a history, because the question of victimhood was put to us directly, for generations, and answered. Tamazgha was colonized, and long colonization hands a people two possible inheritances. One is the grievance worn as an identity, the wound rehearsed until being wronged becomes the national posture and the feelings submerge everything else. That is not the inheritance I received. What I received is the other one: the memory that ordinary people, farmers, shepherds, families with nothing, built the courage to resist, and that the freedom, when it came, was not granted by anyone’s change of heart. It was taken. We freed ourselves, and a people that frees itself learns something about itself that can never be repossessed. You can still touch this. Walk into almost any village in Tamazgha and someone will tell you, without being asked twice, how his grandfather was in the resistance, how he sniped the colonizers from the top of the old kasbah, and he will tell it not as a wound but as an inheritance, the way other families pass down land. Nobody denies the years or what they cost. The difference is what got built on top of them: not the memory of being wronged, the memory of having answered. That is the layer under the underdog reflex, and it is why the doubt of a room has never once read to me as a verdict. My wiring descends from people who heard much harder verdicts than a smirk, and overturned them.

For years I treated the broken part of that wiring as damage to route around. The turn came when I stopped fighting it and started using it, the way you use any strong force you cannot remove: you do not stand in front of it, you point it at something.

The wiring wants a fight where I am not supposed to win. Fine. I feed it those. The fields I had no credentials in, the rooms I was unqualified for, the problems above my weight, every one of them slots perfectly into a reflex my childhood built for free. I could not make the machinery pay me for winning, but I could make it pull for me while I play. That is the first fuel, and I want to be precise about what it is: not pride in a flag, not a story about roots. It is knowing the schematic of your own head well enough to run your work on hardware you did not pick.

The joke test#

The second fuel is curiosity, and everyone says curiosity, so let me say which part of it I actually mean, because it is not the solemn part. It is the fun. The specific, physical fun that fires when I make a joke about an observation. That moment is not decoration on the thinking. It is the thinking. A joke and an insight are the same move performed at different volumes: both of them catch something true from an angle nobody was standing at, and both of them pay out the instant they land. When I can joke about a thing, I know I am finally inside it, because you cannot be witty about a system you do not understand. So I have learned to treat the laugh as an instrument reading. The days the jokes come easily are the days the understanding is moving, and the fun of that, unlike the trophies, pays in a currency my wiring has always accepted.

And almost everything I have built started the same way: an open-ended question asked on nothing but my own intuition, the kind with no assignment behind it and no guarantee there is anything at the bottom. The question is the ignition. Everything else on this list is just what keeps the engine turning after it fires.

The unflattering fuels#

Now the ledger’s uglier column.

I am hardheaded, and a real fraction of my output exists to prove the world wrong. Not the abstract world: specific people, specific rooms, specific sentences said to my face with that specific tone. I wrote in the last piece that I keep no fear because the stake was spent early; what I did not say is what replaced it. Spite is renewable, it never sleeps, and it does not need to be celebrated to keep burning. I know what I am supposed to say about this, that you should let go, that building from resentment poisons the well. Maybe it does for some people. The enemies I made on the road have been some of my most reliable colleagues. They show up every morning without being asked. They never let me ship something weak, because I can hear exactly which of them would smile.

I keep this fuel in a container, because unmanaged it burns the wrong things. The container is a bin I will describe in a moment, and a rule: spite gets a vote on how hard I work, never on what I work on. The direction has to come from the curiosity, or the whole machine turns bitter, and I have watched that happen to better people than me.

The ones I carry#

There is a warmer fuel, and it grows instead of depleting. Somewhere along the road the people around me stopped being only peers and started being, some of them, people I protect: friends, mentees, the younger ones walking a version of the path I walked. I wrote once about the unnamed mentors who showed me doors by standing in them; the debt from that is not payable backward, only forward. So now part of what gets me up is embarrassingly simple: there are people who learn from my failures instead of repeating them, who skip the trap because I have already been in it and drew them the map on the way out. Every scar I have converts into a shortcut for someone I am carrying, and that conversion, scar into map, is the only accounting in which my worst years show a profit. On the days when the curiosity is quiet and the spite is resting, this is the fuel that is still there.

Engineering the emotions instead of obeying them#

Under all of these sits the real discipline, and it is the one the brain writeups have been circling from the start. Emotions are the operating state the thinking runs in; I do not get to delete them, but I refuse to be their employee. Some emotions I have simply learned not to listen to, the way you learn to work in a room with a loud fan. Detaching the work from the feelings about the work is sometimes exactly what keeps the work alive: the disappointment, the flatness after a win, the sting of a rejection, they can all be running in the background while the hands keep moving, because the hands answer to the plan and not to the weather.

But not listening is the crude version. The real version is engineering: learn your own schematic. Know your triggers well enough to see them coming, know which states produce your best work and what reliably induces them, know the early signs of the bad states the way a sailor knows the sky. And then, and this took me longer, learn the schematics of the brains around you, too, because your environment is part of your machine. Rooms have states the same way heads do, and a room can be measured. My favorite instrument is what I call the moon test: tell the room, plainly, that you are going to touch the moon, and watch its face. A room that believes you, or even just refuses to laugh, is a room you can trust with your brain. A room that smirks has told you what it is, and it does not get discarded, nothing gets discarded in this engine: it goes straight into the prove-them-wrong bin, where the spite can metabolize it into schedule.

The state I have to be pulled out of#

One more thing belongs in this inventory, because it is the strongest state I have and the most dangerous, and managing it is half of what motivation means for me now. When the question is good enough, I disappear into the work. Not focus, disappearance: weeks, sometimes months, where I forget meals, forget rest, and eventually forget myself, in the exact sense that the person doing the work stops being someone I am keeping track of. Everything I am proudest of came out of that state. So did every burnout I have ever had. It is one door, and I never know while walking through it which room is on the other side.

I have learned, slowly, to catch myself at the threshold, because once I am inside, the state defends itself: it feels like purpose, it feels like finally, it does not feel like a warning. So the practice is at the entrance, noticing the pull before it becomes the plunge, asking whether this obsession is one I can afford this season. And because I fail at that regularly, I need the last item on the list, the counterweight to every fuel above: a safe environment. A place, a handful of people, where the moon test is already passed and nothing needs proving, where I can turn the brain off and just be. That is not recovery time stolen from the work. It is where the inspiration actually comes from, every time, without exception; the open questions that start everything have never once arrived while I was grinding. And it is the only force I have found that can reach into the disappeared state and pull me back out for an evening, back into being a person, which is the thing the work forgets I am.

So, the answer I owed. What keeps me motivated is an engine built from spare parts: a Tamazgha wiring pointed at new targets, a laugh that doubles as an instrument, a stubbornness with a list of names, a debt paid forward to people I carry, emotions run as controls instead of masters, and two environments, one that doubts me so the engine has something to push against, and one that believes me so the engine has somewhere to rest. None of these parts is happiness. I checked. It runs anyway, and by now I suspect that is not despite the missing part but because everything else had to learn to turn without it.
